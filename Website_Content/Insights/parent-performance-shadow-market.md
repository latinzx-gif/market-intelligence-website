# The Parent Performance Shadow-Market - From Donations to ROI

## What Happened
A structural shift in Thai parental investment logic. Parents are increasingly trading off traditional "school donations" (access fees) in favor of high-intensity "tutoring budgets" and "performance stacks" (supplements). This is driven by extreme information scarcity and admission panic (SIG-032). Additionally, the stress of high-stakes exams (M.1) is triggering a defensive "Early Lock-in" strategy at the P.1 level (SIG-034), pulling cognitive supplement demand down to the 5-7 age range.

## Why It Matters
The supplement is no longer just a "growth vitamin"; it is now a "Strategic Utility" used to maximize the return on expensive tutoring (SIG-033). Furthermore, schools are now evaluating parents based on "Media Regulation" (SIG-036). Supplements that claim to solve the "Digital Deficit" or "Screen Brain" allow parents to signal their alignment with these school standards, turning the product into a proxy for "Good Parenting."

## Strategic Implications
- **Age Range Shift:** Marketing for "Study Performance" should target the 5-7 age group as a proactive "M.1 Avoidance" strategy.
- **Value Proposition:** Positioning must shift from "Long-term Development" to **"Tutoring ROI Enabler"**—helping the child stay alert and focused during expensive evening study sessions.
- **Social Signaling:** Brands can win by providing "Academic Alignment" tools (e.g., focus trackers or screen-time guides) alongside the supplement, reinforcing the parent's status as a responsible academic partner.

## Recommendations
- **R&D (The P.1 Stack):** Develop a "Junior Performance Stack" specifically for the 5-7 age range, focusing on "School-Ready Focus" and "Digital Defense."
- **Marketing Positioning (The Tutor's Ally):** Partner with tutoring centers or use "Study-Session" imagery to position the product as the essential "Fuel" for academic success.
- **Consumer Engagement:** Address the "Admission Panic" by providing clear, data-driven "Performance Benchmarks" that help parents navigate the information vacuum.
